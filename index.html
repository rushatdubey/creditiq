Why CreditIQ exists

Built to make credit risk data legible.

10-year cohort 5,000-borrower PD model CB Ireland verified 2015 to 2024

Irish banks, credit unions, and fintech lenders are managing a shared tension: which borrowers will default if rates stay elevated, where concentration risk is building in LTI and LTV segments, and how the ECB hiking cycle has stressed the book. CreditIQ answers all three using real Central Bank of Ireland data combined with a 5,000-borrower synthetic loan book calibrated to match the actual Irish credit market.

Every figure is traceable to a published Central Bank source. The platform is designed for credit analysts, risk practitioners, and anyone who needs to understand the forces reshaping Irish household and SME lending beyond headline arrears numbers.

Arrears recovery
PDH arrears above 90 days, Q4 each year (2015 to 2024)
Ireland's mortgage arrears rate has fallen from 7.2% in 2015 to 2.8% in Q4 2024, a recovery of 78% from the 12.9% crisis peak in 2013. The pace of improvement slowed during 2020 as COVID payment moratoriums temporarily masked underlying distress.
Arrears composition
Long-term arrears (LTMA) vs short-term, Q4 2024
55% of remaining arrears are long-term (above 720 days), concentrated in non-bank entities. This structural legacy problem is resolving slowly through legal proceedings and restructuring, not organic cure.
Year on year change
Arrears rate change in percentage points, Q4 to Q4
Negative bars indicate improvement. The 2020 COVID bump is clearly visible. Improvement has been consistently steady since 2021 despite the ECB hiking cycle, reflecting strong employment conditions and fixed rate adoption.

Stressed (DTI above 40%) vs comfortable, by rate product
Tracker holders face the highest stress rates due to the ECB hiking cycle direct pass-through. Fixed rate borrowers locked in ahead of the 2022 rate rises show relatively lower stress despite larger loan balances.

The 2022 to 2024 ECB hiking cycle created a €1.2B annual burden on tracker mortgage holders in 2023. The ECB cut in 2024 to 3.15% reduced this to €910M, but 244,000 households remain directly exposed to any future rate increases.

LTI ratio trend
Avg LTI vs share above 3.5x macroprudential limit
The average LTI has risen from 2.85x in 2015 to 3.35x in 2024. The share of new lending above the 3.5x CB limit has grown from 18.2% to 29.2%, building systemic concentration risk in the highest PD segment of the book.

Percentage of new mortgage lending at fixed rate, 2015 to 2024
Fixed rate adoption has tripled from 22% in 2015 to 72% in 2024 as borrowers anticipated and then locked in ahead of the ECB hiking cycle. This insulates most new originations from further rate rises but creates a refinancing cliff as fixed terms expire over 2025 to 2027.
